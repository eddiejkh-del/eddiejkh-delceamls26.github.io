what_i_learned: |
  Today I learned more about the health effects of PM2.5 and the many sources that produce it, especially wildfire smoke. PM2.5 is made up of extremely small particles that can come from vehicle emissions, industrial processes, wood burning, and wildfires. Because these particles are so small, they can travel deep into the lungs and even enter the bloodstream, affecting multiple organs in the body. The CDC explains that wildfire smoke contains a mixture of gases and fine particles that can cause coughing, wheezing, headaches, chest pain, irritated eyes, and difficulty breathing, even in otherwise healthy individuals. People with asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), heart disease, diabetes, pregnant women, children, and older adults are at a much higher risk of experiencing serious health effects from prolonged exposure. These health effects demonstrate why understanding the sources and behavior of PM2.5 is essential for protecting public health.

  Learning about these health risks helped me better understand why monitoring PM2.5 concentrations is so important for public health and environmental protection. It also showed how air pollution is not only an environmental issue but also a significant medical concern that can affect people far away from the original pollution source, such as smoke traveling hundreds of miles from wildfires. I now better understand why air quality alerts encourage people to stay indoors, use air filtration systems, and wear N95 masks during periods of heavy smoke. This lesson connected well with my previous studies on PM2.5 prediction because accurate forecasting can help communities prepare for hazardous air quality events before they occur. Overall, learning about the health impacts of PM2.5 reinforced the importance of developing reliable air quality monitoring and prediction systems to reduce exposure and protect vulnerable populations.
