what_i_learned: |
 Today I learned about the different versions of the YOLO (You Only Look Once) object detection models, specifically YOLOv8 through YOLOv11, and how each version introduces improvements in speed, accuracy, and overall performance. I learned that these models are commonly used for real-time object detection tasks and can be applied to many computer vision applications. Team 2 is evaluating multiple YOLO models to determine which version provides the best balance between detection accuracy, computational efficiency, and inference speed for their project. Comparing the models allows them to identify the strengths and weaknesses of each version before selecting one for further development. I also learned that choosing the most appropriate model is important because it directly impacts the reliability and performance of the final system. Seeing their evaluation process helped me understand that model selection involves testing and comparing results rather than simply choosing the newest version.

 I also learned about Team 4’s project, which uses ECG (electrocardiogram) data and PECS-related features to classify whether patient symptoms are benign or non-benign within their dataset. Their work demonstrated how machine learning can assist in medical decision-making by analyzing physiological data to identify patterns that may not be immediately apparent. I learned that data preprocessing, feature selection, and model evaluation are critical steps for building an accurate classification system in healthcare applications. Their approach showed the importance of using reliable datasets and carefully validating model performance to reduce the chances of incorrect predictions. Overall, today’s presentations gave me a better understanding of how different machine learning models are selected and evaluated for specific applications, from computer vision with YOLO models to medical classification using ECG and related clinical data.
