what_i_learned: |
 Today, I learned about the growing role of deep learning in predicting air pollution, particularly PM2.5 concentrations. Unlike traditional machine learning algorithms, deep learning models such as Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are capable of learning complex nonlinear relationships from large environmental datasets. I also learned that these models can process historical air quality measurements, meteorological variables, and satellite-derived Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD) data to improve prediction accuracy. Several of the research papers demonstrated that combining deep learning with ensemble methods can outperform individual machine learning models. In addition, I learned how hybrid models integrate statistical methods, machine learning, and deep learning to address both temporal forecasting and spatial interpolation. These approaches are particularly useful for predicting pollution levels in areas where monitoring stations are limited. Overall, deep learning provides a powerful approach for modeling the complex patterns that influence air quality.

 I also learned that deep learning models have several limitations despite their high prediction performance. Most deep learning methods require large amounts of high-quality training data and significant computational resources to train effectively. Many studies also emphasized that missing satellite observations, especially AOD data caused by cloud cover, can reduce prediction accuracy. I found it interesting that ensemble frameworks, such as the Deep Ensemble Machine Learning (DEML) model, combine multiple algorithms to improve both accuracy and generalization, although they increase computational complexity. Another important lesson was that feature selection and model interpretability remain critical because understanding which environmental variables influence PM2.5 predictions helps improve both performance and decision-making. Learning about these deep learning techniques has given me a better understanding of how artificial intelligence can be applied to environmental monitoring and has provided ideas that can be incorporated into my own PM2.5 prediction project.
