what_i_learned: |
  Today I learned more about long-term air quality trends in the United States and how government regulations have significantly reduced air pollution over the past several decades. The EPA’s *Our Nation’s Air 2023* report showed that implementation of the Clean Air Act and advances in technology have greatly improved air quality while supporting continued economic growth. Since 1970, emissions of the six major air pollutants have decreased by 78%, even as the U.S. population, economy, and energy use have increased. I also learned that annual and 24-hour PM2.5 concentrations have declined by 42% since 2000, demonstrating the effectiveness of emission control policies. Despite this progress, the report notes that wildfires and changing weather conditions can still cause temporary increases in particle pollution, affecting air quality in many regions. These findings showed me that maintaining clean air requires both effective environmental policies and continuous monitoring. ([gispub.epa.gov][1])

  Another concept I found interesting was how PM2.5 is made up of different components, including elemental carbon, organic carbon, sulfate, nitrate, and crustal materials such as soil and ash. The EPA explained that studying the composition of PM2.5 helps scientists identify pollution sources and evaluate how well pollution control strategies are working. I also learned that analyzing PM2.5 composition supports research on public health, regional visibility, ecosystems, and climate impacts. Although national air quality has improved significantly, millions of Americans still live in areas that exceed one or more national air quality standards. This demonstrates that reducing air pollution is an ongoing effort that requires continued monitoring, research, and technological improvements. Overall, this article helped me better understand the progress made in improving air quality while highlighting the importance of continued PM2.5 monitoring and analysis. ([gispub.epa.gov][1])
