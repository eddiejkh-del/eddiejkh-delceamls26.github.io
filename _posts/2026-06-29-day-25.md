what_i_learned: |
 Today I learned about Support Vector Machines (SVMs and how they can be used for classification and regression tasks by finding the optimal boundary that separates data into different categories. I also learned about the Douglas Optimization Algorithm from Group 8, whose project is closely related to my own. Like my project, their work focuses on PM2.5, but instead of predicting air pollution levels directly, they are training machine learning models to identify communities that are at high risk from prolonged exposure to PM2.5. Seeing how another team approaches a similar problem helped me understand that the same environmental data can be used for different objectives depending on the project's goals. It also gave me new ideas about how optimization techniques can improve the efficiency and accuracy of predictive models. Comparing our projects allowed me to think about alternative ways to analyze air quality data and evaluate model performance.

  In addition, I learned about GenePT, QSAR, and xBD, which introduced me to different applications of artificial intelligence and data science beyond environmental monitoring. GenePT demonstrated how machine learning can be applied to biological and genetic data, while QSAR showed how computational models can predict the properties and behaviors of chemical compounds. I also learned that xBD is a disaster damage assessment dataset that is commonly used to train computer vision models for identifying building damage after natural disasters. These topics expanded my understanding of how AI can solve problems across multiple disciplines, including healthcare, chemistry, and disaster response. Learning about these different datasets and modeling techniques reinforced the importance of selecting the right data and algorithms for a specific application. Overall, today's presentations helped me broaden my knowledge of machine learning while providing valuable insights that I can apply to improving my own PM2.5 prediction project.
