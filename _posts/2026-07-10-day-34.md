what_i_learned: |
 Today I learned more about was mutual information in the context of machine learning and gained a deeper understanding of its technical aspects. I learned that mutual information is a statistical measure that quantifies how much information one variable provides about another. In machine learning, it is commonly used for feature selection because it measures the dependency between input features and the target variable without assuming a linear relationship. This allows it to identify features that contain meaningful predictive information, even when the relationship is nonlinear. I also learned that features with higher mutual information scores are generally more useful for making accurate predictions, while features with low scores often contribute little to the model's performance.

  In addition, I learned more about how mutual information is calculated by comparing the uncertainty of the target variable before and after observing a feature. The greater the reduction in uncertainty, the higher the mutual information score assigned to that feature. This makes mutual information particularly useful for preprocessing datasets by selecting the most informative variables and removing less relevant ones. I also learned that using mutual information can help reduce the dimensionality of a dataset, decrease computational complexity, and improve model performance by minimizing noise. Overall, today's lesson gave me a much better understanding of why mutual information is an important feature engineering and feature selection technique in machine learning workflows.
