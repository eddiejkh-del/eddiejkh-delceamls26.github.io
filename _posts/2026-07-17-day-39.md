what_i_learned: |
  Today’s heavy smog provided a real-world demonstration of the impact that fine particulate matter, or PM2.5, can have on the atmosphere and everyday life. Seeing the sky covered in haze made it much easier to understand how aerosols contribute to reduced visibility and degraded air quality. One of the most striking observations was how the sun appeared deep red instead of its usual bright yellow. This occurred because the dense concentration of airborne particles scattered and absorbed much of the sunlight before it reached the ground. As a result, the sunlight appeared dimmer, and the surrounding environment took on a hazy appearance. Experiencing these conditions firsthand reinforced the connection between PM2.5 concentrations and aerosol particles in the atmosphere.

  This event also highlighted why monitoring PM2.5 is so important for both environmental research and public health. High concentrations of these microscopic particles can travel long distances, reducing visibility while also posing respiratory and cardiovascular health risks. The smog demonstrated that aerosols influence not only the air we breathe but also how solar radiation interacts with the Earth's atmosphere. Observing the red sun and weakened sunlight transformed concepts that are often discussed in textbooks into a tangible, real-world example. Overall, today's conditions strengthened my understanding of the relationship between PM2.5, aerosols, atmospheric scattering, and the importance of accurately predicting air quality.
