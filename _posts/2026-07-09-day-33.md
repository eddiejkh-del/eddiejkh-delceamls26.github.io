what_i_learned: |
 What I learned today was by reading the IEEE paper and the MDPI paper on time-series forecasting, which expanded and reinforced my foundational knowledge of lagging and rolling features that I had previously learned. Before reading these papers, I understood that lagging involved using past observations as input features and that rolling features summarized historical data using statistics such as the mean, standard deviation, minimum, and maximum over a moving window. The IEEE paper further explained that lagging is not simply shifting data backward, but rather selecting the most informative historical observations that can best represent the relationship between past and future values for forecasting tasks. It emphasized that choosing the appropriate lag values is one of the most important steps in developing an accurate time-series forecasting model because different datasets require different historical contexts. The MDPI paper similarly described lag selection as a critical component of feature engineering and demonstrated that selecting the optimal number of previous observations can significantly improve prediction accuracy. The authors proposed combining Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks with heuristic optimization techniques to automatically determine the best lag values instead of relying solely on manual selection or statistical methods. This helped me better understand that lagging is not just a preprocessing step but a feature engineering technique that directly influences how well a machine learning model captures temporal dependencies. 

  In addition to strengthening my understanding of lagging, these papers also reinforced the importance of rolling features for time-series forecasting. Rolling features calculate statistical summaries, such as rolling averages, variances, minimums, and maximums, over a fixed moving window, allowing models to capture short-term trends, seasonal behavior, and fluctuations while reducing the impact of random noise. I learned that these rolling statistics provide valuable contextual information that individual lag values alone cannot capture, making them useful for identifying local patterns within the data. Together, lagging and rolling features create a richer representation of historical information by preserving both individual past observations and aggregated trends. Reading these research papers helped me see why feature engineering is often one of the most important stages in building machine learning models for time-series forecasting. It also showed me that carefully selecting lag values and rolling window sizes can have a significant impact on forecasting performance, especially for environmental datasets such as PM2.5 prediction. Overall, these papers expanded my understanding beyond the basic definitions I previously knew and demonstrated how researchers apply lagging and rolling techniques in real-world forecasting systems to improve model accuracy, robustness, and generalization.
